Exa builds the search engine for AI. Not search for people typing queries into a box — search for models and agents that need clean, relevant web results to reason over. The company started as Metaphor Systems, came out of Y Combinator, and rebranded to Exa in early 2024. The pitch is narrow and technical: web search rebuilt from the index up for LLMs and agents, delivered as an API that developers wire into their own products.

They're respected for two reasons. First, the problem is genuinely hard — crawling, indexing, and ranking the web at scale is infrastructure work, not a wrapper on someone else's API. Second, they raised an $85M Series B in 2025 to keep building it, which put them in the tier of AI-infra companies that can pay competitively and hire slowly on purpose. The bar shows up in who they bring on: small teams, high ownership, engineers who can hold a whole system in their head.

How they hire reflects that. Exa is a San Francisco company that expects you in San Francisco. The team is small enough that founders and team leads are still close to most hiring decisions, and the process rewards people who can show real work rather than talk about it. If you want a big-company ladder with a defined interview rubric and a recruiter shepherding you through six weeks of stages, this is not that.

## Who they're hiring

They're hiring their financial and operational spine at once: VP of Finance, Head of People, Chief of Staff, People Operations, and a Head of Revenue Systems & Technology. Those are the roles you open when you're turning an engineering-led startup into a company.

"Founding" in a title here is literal — the sales motion is early, and you'd be defining it.

Engineering and research stay deep and specialized: Software Engineers across Knowledge Systems, Distributed Data Systems, Web Crawling, Infrastructure, Backend, Full Stack, and Developer Relations, plus a Growth Engineer and a Forward Deployed Engineer. Research roles cover ML and Evals. There's also a creative pocket — Brand Designer and Creative Video Producer. Everything is San Francisco except the NYC-based AE seat.

## The process

I want to be honest about what's verifiable here: Exa doesn't publish a stage-by-stage loop, and candidate reports are thin and vary by team. So treat the following as the shape, not a script.

Expect it to start with a recruiter or hiring-manager screen, then move quickly into substance. For engineering and research, plan for a technical conversation or work sample that probes real depth in your area — systems design and code that reflects how you actually build, not memorized algorithm puzzles. For GTM and operations roles, expect to talk through how you'd stand up a function from close to zero, because in most cases you would be. Because the team is small, you'll likely meet founders or team leads directly, and their read matters more than any scorecard.

Timelines are not well documented and depend on the role and how decisive the team is that week. Ask your recruiter for the specific loop up front — at a company this size, they'll usually just tell you.

## What they value

**Ownership over scope.** Small teams mean you carry a problem end to end. The best fit is someone who'd rather own the whole crawler pipeline than be handed a ticket.

**Depth in a hard domain.** Exa is infrastructure. They value people who go deep — distributed systems, information retrieval, ranking, ML — and can defend their choices.

**Taste and speed together.** Notion-grade product sense paired with the willingness to ship fast and fix later. They're not looking for people who need three planning meetings to start.

**Selling a technical product to technical buyers.** For GTM roles, the buyer is often a developer or an AI team. If you can't get credible about what the API does and why it's better, the pitch falls flat.

**Who is not a fit:** people who want remote work, people who need heavy process and clear ladders, and narrow specialists who won't touch adjacent parts of the stack. If "that's not my job" is in your vocabulary, skip it.

## The technical bar

It varies sharply by function. Search, infrastructure, crawling, and distributed data roles expect real systems depth — you should be comfortable reasoning about scale, latency, and data pipelines that run across the web. Research (ML, Evals) leans toward people with genuine ML or IR backgrounds who can design and measure, not just fine-tune. Forward Deployed and Growth Engineers need product and customer instincts on top of solid engineering — you're closer to users. Full Stack and Dev Rel want breadth and the ability to make the API pleasant to build against. Across all of them, the bar is "show me," not credentials.

## Things worth knowing

A referral matters more here than at a big company, because the team is small and trusts its own network. If you know someone inside, use them.

Equity is a real part of the compensation story at this stage, and the Series B gives the option pool weight — but early-stage equity is a bet, so size the offer on cash plus your own read of the company's odds.

Growth trajectory is the appeal: joining now means joining before the org fully solidifies, which is where "founding" titles and outsized ownership actually exist. That upside comes with ambiguity. You'll build the process, not inherit it.

## Should you apply?

Apply if you want to work in-person in San Francisco on hard infrastructure or a founding-stage function, and you'd rather own a problem than a narrow scope. The company is well-funded, technically serious, and early enough that your work is visible. Don't apply if you need remote flexibility, defined ladders, or a mature playbook to follow. If the ambiguity reads as opportunity rather than risk to you, this is a strong bet — go in ready to show real work and ask exactly what the loop looks like before you start.
