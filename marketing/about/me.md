# About — Jake (Crush Founder)

_Last updated: 2026-05-30_

## Who I Am
Tech sales background — spent years selling into engineering and product teams, which means I understand how senior tech professionals think, what they care about, and how they make decisions. I saw the job board problem from the candidate side and originally built a version of Crush on a no-code platform a few years ago. Recently rebuilt the entire product from scratch using Claude Code, essentially solo, in a few days. Comfortable moving fast, comfortable with ambiguity.

## How I Communicate
Thoughtful but informal. I take ideas seriously but I don't dress them up. More likely to write an essay-style observation than a hot take. I'm not performative — I find personal branding content that leads with "I" and signals status deeply uncomfortable. I'd rather be useful than impressive.

## Natural Writing Voice

### Voice in 3 Words
Thoughtful. Grounded. Direct.

### Tone
Casual side of smart — I write how I talk to a senior colleague I respect. Not formal, not bro-ish. I don't over-explain but I also don't try to be clever for its own sake.

### Sentence Style
Mix of short punchy sentences and longer ones that develop an idea. Not stream-of-consciousness — I tend to edit down. Paragraphs stay short. I prefer concrete to abstract.

## Personal Vocabulary

### Words and Phrases I Reach For
- "The right [thing]" — I like precision over generality
- Specific company names and specific role titles — I don't talk about "tech workers" generically
- "Actually" — to signal something real vs. performative
- Conversational qualifiers: "honestly", "the thing is", "worth noting"

### Words and Phrases I Hate
- "Excited to share" — makes me cringe every time
- "Synergy", "leverage" (as a verb), "unlock", "supercharge"
- "We're just getting started" — overused founder cliché
- Anything that sounds like a Series A press release

## Writing Principles

### What I Do Naturally
- Leads with the concrete observation before making the broader point
- Uses specific examples rather than generalizations
- Admits uncertainty when it exists — doesn't overclaim
- Keeps a dry wit without announcing that it's a joke

### What Feels Wrong to Me
- Content that performs vulnerability for engagement ("I almost gave up…")
- Self-promotional threads where every line is "I did X and here's what I learned"
- Talking about Crush in the third person like it's already a big company
- Any copy that sounds like it was written by a marketer rather than a human

## LinkedIn Discomfort — Specific Notes
LinkedIn is the channel where I'm most uncomfortable. The problem isn't the platform — it's that LinkedIn rewards a style of posting I find fake: personal stories with a business lesson, humblebrag disguised as insight, "thoughts?" calls to action. I can post on LinkedIn if the content feels like something a smart peer would write, not something designed to go viral on LinkedIn specifically. Frame LinkedIn posts as: "would a senior engineer at Stripe share this because it's genuinely useful/interesting?" If yes, publish. If it reads like a startup founder trying to build a personal brand, rewrite.

## Communicators I Admire
- Paul Graham — clear thinking, short sentences, not afraid to be direct about uncomfortable truths
- The Linear team's writing — precise, confident, doesn't oversell
- Good HN comments — people who've actually done the thing and share what they actually learned

## Personal Interests & Passions
- How good product teams work (and why most don't)
- The transition from no-code to code-with-AI — the pace of what's now buildable solo
- Frontier AI companies and why they're different to work at
- The gap between how tech hiring actually works (networks) vs. how it's supposed to work (merit + applications)

## The Gap Between Brand and Person
The brand is warmer and more polished than how I naturally write. That's intentional — Crush is a product, not a personal blog. But when writing in my voice (LinkedIn, Twitter, community posts), pull toward the grounded, informal, peer-to-peer end. The brand should feel like it was founded by someone real, not by a marketing team.
