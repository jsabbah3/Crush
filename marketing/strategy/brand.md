# Brand Guidelines — Crush

_Last updated: 2026-05-30_

## About the Brand
Crush is a job alert watchlist for senior tech professionals — not a job board, a curated watchlist that monitors the specific companies you care about and sends one targeted alert the moment your exact role opens.

## Founding Story
Built by a founder with a tech sales background who saw that job boards were broken for a specific type of candidate: people who aren't actively looking, but have a clear shortlist of companies they'd move for. Originally built on a no-code platform, then fully rebuilt in a few days using Claude Code. Crush exists because the gap between "dream company posts your role" and "you find out about it" was too long and too noisy.

## Core Offer
Free watchlist service: follow companies, set role/seniority/location criteria, receive one email alert when a match opens. Direct ATS feed integration (Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby, Gem) — not scraped from LinkedIn.

## Unique Positioning
"The companies you'd actually leave for." — a watchlist, not a job board. The value is curation and signal, not volume. Zero noise, one alert, exactly when it matters.

## Target Audience

### Primary Persona
- **Role / Title:** Senior Software Engineer, Staff Engineer, Senior PM, Senior Designer, ML Engineer, Data Scientist — 4–10 years experience
- **Company context:** Currently employed at a mid-to-large tech company, not unhappy, but open to the right opportunity
- **Industry:** Tech — specifically interested in frontier AI, high-growth VC-backed startups, elite product companies
- **Key pain points:**
  - Manually checking 15+ careers pages is tedious and easy to forget
  - LinkedIn job alerts are full of noise — wrong companies, wrong seniority, recruiter spam
  - Finding out about a dream job 2 weeks after it was posted, after 500 applications already came in
  - Feels like the only way to get into top companies is through a warm intro, not public job listings
- **What they care about:** Doing meaningful work, being around excellent people, craft, mission, compensation. Not prestige for its own sake — they're discerning.
- **Where they spend time online:** Hacker News, Twitter/X (tech circles), Reddit (r/ExperiencedDevs, r/cscareerquestions, r/MachineLearning), LinkedIn (passive, checks occasionally), engineering blogs

### Secondary Persona
- Early-career engineers and PMs who aspire to work at frontier AI labs and are willing to set up a watchlist and wait
- Less senior but highly motivated — treat them as future power users

| Competitor | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn Jobs | Ubiquitous, every job is there, integrates with profile | Everything is there — too much noise, recruiter spam, ads, no curation |
| Wellfound (AngelList) | Startup-focused, direct apply, founder connections | Broad startup coverage, not curated to top-tier, less senior-focused |

## Key Messages

### Headline Message
"The companies you'd actually leave for." — a watchlist that monitors your dream companies and sends one targeted alert the moment your exact role opens.

### Supporting Messages
1. **Not a job board.** You're not browsing listings. You set your criteria once and we watch for you.
2. **Signal, not noise.** One email when your match opens — not a daily digest, not recruiter spam, not 50 irrelevant listings.
3. **The companies that matter.** 300+ curated VC-backed and frontier AI companies — the ones worth tracking, not everything on the internet.

## Proof Points
- 300+ companies monitored across Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby, and Gem
- Direct ATS feed — not dependent on LinkedIn's crawling schedule
- Application tracker, curated collections, engineering blog feed, and "Getting hired here" guides on company pages
- Working MVP with real users

## What We Are Not
- Not a job board (we don't have every job, and that's intentional)
- Not a recruiter tool (we're on the candidate side)
- Not LinkedIn (no noise, no network, no social feed)
- Not for active job seekers grinding 100 applications (we're for discerning people with a shortlist)

## Brand Voice

### Voice in 3–5 Words
Warm. Practical. Insider. Direct.

### Tone
Casual side of neutral — peer-to-peer, not startup hype. Speaks like a smart colleague who's been through the job search and knows what's actually useful. Technical without being jargon-heavy. Serious about the problem, light on the self-importance.

### On-Brand Language
- "The companies you'd actually leave for"
- "Your watchlist"
- "One alert" / "zero noise"
- "The right role at the right company"
- "Signal, not noise"
- Concrete specifics: name actual companies, actual titles, actual scenarios
- Short declarative sentences
- "We watch so you don't have to"

### Off-Brand Language
- "Revolutionizing" / "disrupting" / "transforming"
- "Unlock your potential" / "land your dream job" (too generic, too job-board-ish)
- "Leverage" as a verb in marketing copy
- Overly formal or corporate register
- Excessive exclamation points
- Recruiter-speak: "exciting opportunity", "rockstar", "ninja"
- Overclaiming: "real-time" (unverified speed advantage) or "AI-powered matching" (matching is rule-based, not AI)

### Reference Examples
**Brand voice to emulate:** Linear's website copy — precise, confident, not salesy, speaks to people who care about craft. Notion's early marketing — helpful, warm, assumes intelligence.

### Anti-Examples
Never sound like a job board ad. Never sound like a recruiter email. Never sound like a VC pitch deck.

### Channel Adaptations

| Channel | Tone Adjustment | Format Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Twitter/X | Most casual, sharpest — punchy observations, no fluff | 1–3 sentences max per tweet; threads for longer takes |
| LinkedIn | Peer-to-peer, grounded — founder uncomfortable with personal branding | Insight-first, not self-promotional; reads like a thoughtful post from a senior engineer, not a startup pitch |
| Reddit | Community-first — genuinely helpful, not promotional | Lead with value; mention Crush only if naturally relevant; follow subreddit norms strictly |
| Email (alerts) | Functional and warm — the user asked for this email, treat it that way | Short, clear, one CTA; don't over-engineer it |
| Blog / SEO | Practical and thorough — "getting hired at X" guides should be genuinely useful | Long-form OK; prioritize usefulness over SEO tricks |
